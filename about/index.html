Concepts

Concept A

Primary hypothesis: A denser, list-based navigation without separate L1/2 panels saves space on screen and improves time to navigation, discoverability, and findability while providing a clear visual map of our product.

  • QB customers who use the product regularly develop a mental map of where things live. The current vertical stack underserves that map by hiding top level nav items (apps) behind progressive disclosure.
  • A dense, always-visible list gives experienced customers a complete, scannable picture of the product (clear spatial memory) in a single glance (much like our create new fly-out).
  • Reducing the number of interactions required to reach any destination (no panel opens, no page transitions to orient) shortens task time and increases confidence.
  • This is a much more scalable solution if our L0s ever grow.

Concept B

Primary hypothesis: Our customers don't navigate to features but to modes. Before they look for "recurring invoices" they decide they're in Sales mode. A hub/spoke approach that reflects this mental model by helping ground them in context in a dedicated “mode view" before committing to a sub-task, which will reduce “wrong turns” and create natural discovery moments for adjacent features.

  • In hub/spoke navigation, the L0 list acts as a launching pad, not a destination. Clicking an L0 item opens a dedicated panel (like a mini-hub) that surfaces L1/2 options, recently visited items, shortcuts, and contextual entry points for that domain.
  • Customers don't just need to find features, they need to orient within a domain before committing to a sub-task, and a panel gives them that moment.
  • This mirrors how customers think about their business: "I'm going into Sales mode" before "I need to find the recurring invoices setting."

Concept C

Primary hypothesis: Our customers know the outcome they need, yet we ask them to name the category that outcome lives in. An AI layer that routes "I need to see X, Y, Z" directly to the right object removes the need for customers to learn our taxonomy and affords us a minimalistic approach to navigation—because our navigation failure might not be a wayfinding problem, but an intent-matching problem.
